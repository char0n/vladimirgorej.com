<p class="lead">
  <i>Developer Marketing Does Not Exist</i> by Adam Duvander makes a deceptively simple argument: if you want to reach developers, stop thinking of yourself as a marketer and start thinking of yourself as an educator. "Marketer" may be in your title, but reframing toward education is what gives you the mindset to attract a technical audience.
</p>

The core message is **share knowledge, not features**. Developers find you through problem-focused content, so the book insists you keep topics centered on developer problems rather than your particular solution. Your home page, your getting started guide, your tutorials should all speak to a technical problem first, and let your product ride along in its draft.

Duvander is at his most practical when breaking down content structure. He treats the blog like a real publication with an editorial calendar and a defined cadence (somewhere between two posts per week and two per month), and argues convincingly that a few great articles outperform a pile of low-quality ones. He also lays out an ideal tutorial structure: explain the context, show the end result, walk through the steps, and help the reader take the next step. Tutorials are then set apart from deeper **guides**, the "Signature Content" of a developer company. Competitors can copy your product, but not your understanding of the problem.

The chapter I found most immediately useful was the **DX Index**: 13 concrete criteria for assessing developer experience, covering everything from documentation and pricing to sample applications and example cURL calls. It's a checklist you can actually score yourself against, and the advice to fix the biggest gaps first is refreshingly grounded.

Other sections broadened my view of the available channels: developer tools as marketing (with API Transformer by APIMatic as a nicely chosen example), open sourcing your SDKs and documentation, and treating sponsorships as long-term partnerships rather than quick growth tactics. Throughout, the throughline holds: shift the focus away from your product, and you create a strange gravity that pulls developers toward you.

Coming from an API tooling and governance background, I also appreciated how many of the book's examples revolve around API companies and API products. The problems Duvander uses to illustrate his points, from developer portals and getting started guides to SDKs and API references, are exactly the ones I work with, which made the advice feel concrete rather than abstract.

**Verdict:** I already had a rough sense of what developer marketing involves, but I had never read a properly comprehensive, structured treatment of it. This book verified what I already knew and expanded my horizon toward more channels than I was aware of. It's a good read and worth every penny.
